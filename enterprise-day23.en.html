Day 23 · Business Cycles & the Companies That Led

The Market-Makers: Four Companies That Each Rewrote the Rules

Sunday, July 19, 2026 · BigCat's Time Machine
Great companies often win not by doing the same thing better, but by changing the dimension of competition itself: Wedgwood invented the "brand," Rockefeller invented "scale," Sony invented "making people want what doesn't yet exist," and Toyota, in a crisis, honed "less waste" into a blade. The business cycle is the coldest judge of all — it periodically topples the last round's winners, keeping the throne only for those who can rewrite the rules.
EVENT · 01

Wedgwood: He Didn't Make Pottery Cheaper — He Invented "Wanting"Josiah Wedgwood & the Birth of the Brand · 1765

1765Staffordshire, EnglandInvention of the Brand
Background & Key Figures

Mid-18th-century England was crowded with pottery workshops churning out near-identical wares that could only compete on price. Josiah Wedgwood (1730–1795), born into a family of potters, was left with a leg that could not work the wheel after childhood smallpox — forced away from the throwing bench toward recipes and factory management. That "defect" turned a craftsman into a businessman.

What Happened

In 1765 he made a tea service for Queen Charlotte and won the right to call such ware "Queen's Ware" — royal endorsement, instantly a marketing badge. He opened a London showroom, ran direct-mail catalogues, offered free delivery and high prices later cut selectively. He split the production process so each worker specialized in one step (a practice predating Adam Smith's pin-factory parable). The core strategy was desire from the top down: sell first to aristocrats to manufacture the craving to "imitate upward," then mass-produce for the middle class. He wasn't selling pottery; he was selling status.

Counterfactual + Historians' Debate

Nancy Koehn's "Brand New" (2001) treats Wedgwood as one of the inventors of the "brand" concept: in an information-scarce age, a trusted name is itself an asset. Counterfactual: without the lever of royal endorsement, pottery of equal quality would likely have been dragged into a price war and lost in the crowd. The debate: was he a product innovator or a marketing illusionist? Most historians see the two as inseparable — he made the pottery genuinely good first, which is what let the marketing cash its promises.

Modern Parallel

Apple's limited launches, its stores, its "Designed by Apple" — implant desire in the taste-makers first, then mass-produce for everyone — follow Wedgwood's logic exactly. Two centuries on, "manufacturing want" still pays better than "meeting need."

One-Line Lesson + Question
The real moat is often not making things cheaper but making people want them; manufacturing desire pays better than satisfying need.
When products are near-identical, do you keep fighting the price war, or redefine what people are actually buying?
EVENT · 02

Rockefeller: Turning Chaos into Order — and Order into MonopolyRockefeller, Standard Oil & the Rise of Scale · 1870–1911

1870–1911United StatesScale & Monopoly
Background & Key Figures

The 1860s American oil business was a speculative frenzy: drillers boomed and went bust, refiners over-competed, prices whipsawed. John D. Rockefeller (1839–1937) — calm, calculating, allergic to waste — refused to gamble on drilling. He fixed on the least glamorous but most decisive link: refining and transport.

What Happened

He founded Standard Oil in 1870. His methods were ruthless and systematic: negotiate secret rebates with railroads to cut freight costs, force rivals to submit, then absorb them one by one through horizontal integration; in 1882 he pioneered the "trust" as an organizational form to unify control. By the 1880s Standard controlled roughly 90% of U.S. refining capacity. He turned oil from a gamble into a standardized, reliably supplied industrial good — "Standard" meant standard. But Ida Tarbell's investigative reporting exposed the machinery and stoked public fury. In 1911 the Supreme Court, under the Sherman Antitrust Act, broke it into 34 companies (the ancestors of Exxon, Mobil, and Chevron).

Counterfactual + Historians' Debate

Ron Chernow's "Titan" (1998) and Alfred Chandler's "The Visible Hand" (1977) both cast Standard Oil as the prototype of the "modern managerial enterprise" — replacing the disorder of the market with organization and scale. Counterfactual: without the breakup, American capitalism might have slid toward permanent monopoly; it was precisely the breakup that established the principle that "scale has limits." The debate: was he a predatory monopolist, or a builder who tamed a savage industry into a civilized one?

Modern Parallel

Today's antitrust fights over tech giants (Google, Amazon) are almost a replay of the Standard Oil script: scale brings efficiency and low prices, and also strangles competition. Society still has no consensus on where to draw the line.

One-Line Lesson + Question
Scale is both the source of efficiency and the breeding ground of monopoly; the same force that lowers costs can also smother competition.
When a company grows large enough to define an entire industry, should we praise its efficiency or fear its power?
EVENT · 03

Sony: Turning Down a 100,000-Unit Order to Sell What Didn't ExistSony: Selling What Doesn't Yet Exist · 1946–1979

1946–1979TokyoInnovation & Brand
Background & Key Figures

In 1946, Masaru Ibuka and Akio Morita founded Tokyo Tsushin Kogyo in the ruins of postwar Tokyo. "Made in Japan" then meant cheap and shoddy. The two bet the opposite: that original technology and their own brand could build global trust out of rubble.

What Happened

In 1955 the company licensed Bell Labs' transistor patent and built a pocket-sized transistor radio, shrinking a bulky appliance into a shirt pocket. Around then the American watchmaker Bulova offered a 100,000-unit contract to manufacture radios under Bulova's name — and Morita refused: better to lose the order than surrender the brand "Sony." It looked insane, and it became Sony's defining stand. The Trinitron television and, in 1979, the Walkman followed. Morita did no market research, because "consumers don't know what they want" — the Walkman conjured, out of nothing, the entirely new desire to carry your music with you.

Counterfactual + Historians' Debate

John Nathan's "Sony: The Private Life" (1999) records the rejection of the Bulova order as Sony's pivotal choice. Counterfactual: had they taken that contract, Sony might well have become just another invisible contract manufacturer, never earning a name of its own. The debate: was the Walkman's "build the product first, then create the demand" genius intuition, or survivorship bias — how many research-free gambles failed and left no one to remember them?

Modern Parallel

Steve Jobs's line that "consumers don't know what they want" is almost a copy of Morita's. And Sony's later sluggishness in the digital age (missing the MP3 player and the iPod) is its own warning: a company that defined one era need not define the next.

One-Line Lesson + Question
The strongest innovation is not meeting a known need but creating something people only later realize they can't live without — and that is also the hardest to copy and easiest to crash.
Turning down a lucrative contract-manufacturing order to hold onto your own brand — foresight or arrogance? Where is the line?
EVENT · 04

The Oil Shock: One Price Move Reshuffled the Auto IndustryThe 1973 Oil Shock & the Toyota Ascent

Oct 1973GlobalReordering in Crisis
Background & Key Figures

In October 1973, an OPEC oil embargo quadrupled prices within four months. The world's rulers then were Detroit's Big Three — makers of large, thirsty cars sitting comfortably on the throne; Toyota and its Japanese peers were seen as followers churning out cheap small cars.

What Happened

Soaring prices flipped consumer preference overnight: fuel-efficient Japanese compacts were suddenly in demand. But the deeper difference lay in the production system — Toyota's "lean production" (the Toyota Production System led by Taiichi Ohno): just-in-time (JIT), near-zero inventory, continuous improvement (kaizen), letting it respond at low cost, high quality, and high speed. In the crisis, Detroit's high-volume, high-inventory model revealed its rigidity, and Toyota surged into the U.S. market. This was not luck: the oil shock merely pulled the trigger; the real decider was the superiority of one production philosophy over another. By the 1980s Japanese automakers were taking American ground, forcing rivals to turn back and study lean.

Counterfactual + Historians' Debate

James Womack's "The Machine That Changed the World" (1990) coined the term "lean production" and argued the systemic advantage of the Toyota model. Counterfactual: without the 1973 oil shock, Toyota's efficiency edge might have taken another decade-plus to show — the crisis was an accelerant, magnifying an already-existing gap all at once. The debate: was Toyota's rise mainly owed to the external oil shock, or to the internal production revolution? Most scholars hold the latter to be fundamental, the former merely a catalyst.

Modern Parallel

Every macro shock (the 2008 financial crisis, the 2020 pandemic, supply-chain ruptures) reshuffles the industry order — the shock doesn't pick the winner; it exposes whose underlying capability is stronger. Tesla's rise amid incumbents' hesitation follows the same logic.

One-Line Lesson + Question
A crisis doesn't create winners; it reveals early who was already stronger. The cycle is a truth-mirror, exposing the fragility that prosperity concealed.
When the external shock arrives, do you want your organization to be one that "profits in good weather," or one that "seizes share in the storm"? The divide was set before the crisis ever came.

Going Deeper

Q1: All four "rewrote the rules" — is there a shared pattern behind it?
Yes — none of them did better within the existing rules; each changed the dimension of competition. In a price war, Wedgwood shifted to status; in chaos, Rockefeller shifted to organization; amid imitation, Sony shifted to original demand; in prosperity, Toyota quietly honed efficiency. The common move: relocate the fight from the battlefield the rival is good at to one you have redefined. This is the essence of "blue ocean" strategy — the winner is often not whoever tries harder, but whoever changes the board.
Q2: Is scale a blessing or a curse? From Standard Oil to today's tech giants.
Scale is inherently two-faced: it brings efficiency and low prices, and it strangles competition. The historical dilemma of antitrust is that it is hard to draw a clean line between "big and beneficial" and "big and harmful" — the oil industry grew more vibrant after Standard's breakup, yet the breakup itself may have hurt economies of scale. The crux is perhaps not scale but power: the question is not "how big" but "big enough to unilaterally define the rules and lock out newcomers?" When scale becomes the key that locks the door rather than the efficiency that opens it, the red line has arrived.
Q3: Sony defined the analog era but missed the digital one — why is the "winner's curse" so common?
Christensen's "innovator's dilemma" gives the classic answer: the very capabilities, assets, and organizational habits that made you succeed become the shackles on your transformation. Sony held both hardware and content (even its own music company), yet fear of piracy of its own music hobbled it on the MP3 player, and Apple — free of that baggage — cut in behind it. The lesson is harsh: a moat is also a besieged city; yesterday's core competency can be tomorrow's path dependence.
Q4: "Crises reshuffle the order" — why do cycles always reward preparation you couldn't see?
In prosperity, even inefficiency turns a profit, and the market cannot tell "truly strong" from "merely lucky"; a crisis strips away the fig leaf of liquidity and demand, and underlying capability (cost structure, cash, organizational resilience) finally shows. So the real competition happens in the quiet period before the crisis — what you quietly trained while others partied decides how much you can seize while others panic. Toyota's lean was not invented in the crisis; it was forged in the decade-plus before it.
Q5: Counterfactual — without these "individuals," would history differ? Do heroes make the tide, or does the tide summon heroes?
Both are true, but at different levels. The Industrial Revolution was bound to produce some "Wedgwood-like" brand pioneer; the oil boom was bound to summon some integrator of scale — structure determined that "there would be such a person." But exactly who, by what means, and how far they pushed it, is full of contingency and personal imprint. The tide decides where the door opens; the hero decides who rushes through first and how far. Business history is neither pure destiny nor pure genius, but the conspiracy of the two.
