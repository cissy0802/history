Day 17 · Modern Middle East

Four Fault Lines: The Choices That Shaped the Modern Middle East

Monday, July 13, 2026 · BigCat's Time Machine
After the Ottoman Empire collapsed in 1918, almost every border in the Middle East was drawn by outsiders with a ruler — and much of the region's turbulence over the century since has been the interest owed on a few hasty decisions. —— after Eugene Rogan, The Arabs: A History (2009). Each of the four turning points below could once have gone another way.
EVENT · 01

The Founding of Israel: One Day, Two Peoples' Origin and RuptureFounding of Israel · 1948

Nov 1947 UN PartitionMay 14, 1948 StatehoodNakba
Background & Key Figures

Britain's 1917 Balfour Declaration promised a Jewish "national home" in Palestine — while making conflicting promises to the Arabs. Between the wars, Jewish immigration surged amid persecution and the Holocaust, colliding with the local Arab population. In 1947 an exhausted Britain dumped the problem on the UN, whose Resolution 181 proposed partition: a Jewish state, an Arab state, and an internationalized Jerusalem. The Jewish side accepted; the Arab side refused — why, they asked, should half the land be given away when they were the two-thirds majority?

What Happened

On May 14, 1948, David Ben-Gurion declared Israel's independence in Tel Aviv; as British forces withdrew, five Arab states invaded the next day. The first Arab-Israeli war ran nearly a year, and the better-equipped, better-organized Israelis not only held the partition line but seized more territory. Its other face was the Nakba (Catastrophe): roughly 700,000 Palestinian Arabs fled or were driven from their homes, the source of a refugee question unresolved to this day. One and the same war was one nation's epic of independence and another's start of exile.

Counterfactual + Historians' Debate

Israel's "New Historians" — Benny Morris, The Birth of the Palestinian Refugee Problem (1988) — used declassified archives to show the refugees both fled and were expelled, shattering both sides' official myths; Avi Shlaim, The Iron Wall (2000) stresses a tacit Israeli-Jordanian understanding to partition. Counterfactual: had the Arab side accepted partition in 1947? There would have been a Palestinian state — smaller, earlier — and historians disagree over whether it could have survived. The real constraint: two peoples held real, incommensurable claims to the same land, and any plan required someone to pay.

Modern Parallel

When each side's legitimate claims simply cannot both be satisfied, conflict is not a "misunderstanding" and no negotiation is "win-win." Such zero-sum identity conflicts recur today — from territory to fights over resources inside an organization — and resolving them begins with admitting they cannot be resolved cheaply.

Lesson + Question
The hardest conflicts aren't about who is right, but two valid claims colliding on the same ground — someone is bound to pay the price.
Is the dispute you face a misunderstanding that can be win-win, or a zero-sum clash where someone must concede? Tell them apart before you talk solutions.
EVENT · 02

The Iranian Revolution: A "Clerical Takeover" No One ForesawIranian Revolution · 1979

1953 CoupFeb 1979 Khomeini ReturnsTheocracy
Background & Key Figures

The Shah, Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, regained absolute power after a 1953 Anglo-American coup toppled the elected premier Mosaddegh. He pushed his "White Revolution" modernization, but ruled through the SAVAK secret police as inequality widened. The opposition was a motley coalition: secular leftists, liberals, the bazaar merchants, and the exiled Shi'a cleric Ayatollah Khomeini. Their only shared aim — overthrow the Shah.

What Happened

Protests snowballed through 1978, and strikes paralyzed the oil industry. In January 1979 the Shah fled; on February 1 Khomeini returned from Paris to millions lining the streets. The decisive stretch came afterward: a revolution launched by a broad coalition was steadily captured by its most tightly organized, most ideologically certain faction — the clergy. By referendum Iran became an "Islamic Republic" — a theocracy in which a Supreme Leader sits above president and parliament. The secular and leftist allies were soon purged.

Counterfactual + Historians' Debate

Ervand Abrahamian, A History of Modern Iran (2008) frames this as the eruption of accumulated social tensions, not a pure religious revival; the "Islamization" of its outcome was not preordained. Counterfactual: had secular liberals or the left taken power? Iran might have gone another way — but Khomeini's camp won on organization and a clear program that the loose liberals could not match. History repeats the pattern: revolutions are launched by broad coalitions but harvested by the best-organized minority.

Modern Parallel

Tearing down the old order is easy; deciding the new one is hard. In a power vacuum, the winner is rarely the most numerous or most moderate side, but the best-organized with the clearest goal. From political transitions to corporate change, the rule holds.

Lesson + Question
Toppling the old takes a coalition; building the new takes organization — in chaos, a clear and determined minority beats a scattered majority.
In every "change" you join, who is organizing for the "after"? Or is everyone fixated only on the "tearing down"?
EVENT · 03

The Gulf War: How a "Quick Victory" Seeded a Longer WarThe Gulf War · 1990–91

Aug 1990 Invasion of KuwaitJan 1991 Desert StormUnipolar Moment
Background & Key Figures

The 1980–88 Iran-Iraq War dragged on eight years; though Iraq's Saddam Hussein was not formally defeated, he emerged buried in debt with a wrecked economy. He eyed his southern neighbor Kuwait: oil-rich, accused of over-pumping to depress prices, and once part of the Ottoman province of Basra. On August 2, 1990, Iraqi forces overran and annexed Kuwait — Saddam betting the U.S. would not go to war over a small state.

What Happened

He bet wrong. The elder Bush's administration assembled a 34-nation coalition, including several Arab states, with UN authorization. In January 1991 "Desert Storm" opened with an unprecedented air campaign; a ground war of just 100 hours in February drove Iraq out of Kuwait. With the Cold War barely over, this was hailed as a "new world order" and America's "unipolar moment." But Bush deliberately stopped at liberating Kuwait, refusing to march on Baghdad — fearing an occupation quagmire and the coalition's collapse. Saddam survived, seeding the far more consequential war of 2003.

Counterfactual + Historians' Debate

The debate persists: should the 1991 coalition have pressed on to remove Saddam? Most historians see the elder Bush's restraint as prudent — the chaos of the 2003 Iraq War (sectarian civil war, an unwinnable occupation) vindicated the choice not to enter Baghdad. A subtler counterfactual: had U.S. ambassador April Glaspie drawn Saddam a clear red line beforehand, the invasion might have been avoided — ambiguous signals are often misread as consent.

Modern Parallel

A clean, decisive victory may solve only the surface problem while handing the real difficulty to the future. "Winning cleanly" is not "winning completely"; knowing where to stop matters as much as knowing how to win.

Lesson + Question
The hard part isn't winning but judging where to stop — one step too far is a quagmire, one step too short leaves a hidden fuse.
That "total victory" on your desk — did it truly solve the problem, or just postpone the hard part for your future self?
EVENT · 04

The Arab Spring: A Spring a Street Vendor Lit, and That Cooled FastThe Arab Spring · 2011

Dec 2010 Tunisian Self-ImmolationFeb 2011 Mubarak FallsMissing Institutions
Background & Key Figures

On December 17, 2010, the Tunisian street vendor Mohamed Bouazizi — his cart confiscated, humiliated with no recourse — set himself on fire before the town hall. Spread by social media, the act ignited long-pent rage: high unemployment, prices, corruption, and decades of unchanging autocrats. Protests raced across the Arab world like wildfire.

What Happened

On January 14, 2011, Ben Ali — Tunisia's ruler of 23 years — fled; on February 11, Egypt's Mubarak resigned before a million voices in Cairo's Tahrir Square. For a moment the old order seemed poised to fall everywhere. But the "spring" chilled fast: Egypt's military retook power two years later; Libya, Syria, and Yemen slid into brutal civil war and foreign intervention. Only Tunisia — where it began — struggled toward a democratic transition (with later reversals of its own). Why did such similar sparks yield such divergent ends?

Counterfactual + Historians' Debate

Fawaz Gerges, Making the Arab World (2018) and others argue that toppling a dictator is only step one; what really decides the outcome is the underlying stock of institutions and civil society: Tunisia had relatively independent unions and an army that stayed out of politics; Egypt's military, with its entangled economic interests, would never truly cede power. Counterfactual: had Egypt enjoyed Tunisia-style civil-military relations, the transition might have held. The bleak lesson — the cheering in a square can topple a man, but cannot manufacture an institution. A revolution's fate is mostly set by the social structure built up in the decades before the uprising.

Modern Parallel

Removing a bad leader is far from building a good system. Whether in a nation, a company, or a community, real resilience lives in institutions, rules, and intermediary bodies — not in whether one person stays or goes. Passion can tear down; only structure can rebuild.

Lesson + Question
Toppling a person takes a burst of anger; building an institution takes decades of accumulation — the square's cheer is a start, not an end.
When you think "just replace that person and the problem is solved" — is what's really missing the institution no one is building?

Four Turning Points, One Common Thread

The modern Middle East keeps snagging on the same spot: breaking the old order is easy, but the "institutional foundation" of the new order is slow to rise.

Deeper Thinking

Q1: Do the four turning points really share a "breaking is easy, building is hard" thread?
Read the table as one mechanism: the modern Middle East cycles through "break the old order — struggle to build the new." The founding ended the Mandate but left refugees; the revolution ended monarchy but led to theocracy; the Gulf War won yet stopped short, leaving a fuse; the Arab Spring toppled autocrats but could not build institutions. The common thread isn't "who was right," but that destruction is instantaneous while construction needs decades of accumulated institutions, civil society, and trust — precisely what this land has most lacked.
Q2: Iran and Tunisia — why did their revolutions end so differently?
Both were launched by broad coalitions. Iran's was harvested by its best-organized faction, the clergy, ending in theocracy; Tunisia had relatively independent unions and an apolitical army, and struggled toward democracy. The decisive variable was the social structure built up over the decades before the uprising — who was best organized, how the army's economic interests were bound, whether intermediary bodies existed. The lesson: a revolution's outcome is largely seeded in the soil long before it erupts.
Q3: The elder Bush stopping short of Baghdad in 1991 — wise or weak?
The chaos of the 2003 Iraq War (sectarian civil war, an unwinnable occupation, a destabilized region) acts like a cruel control experiment that vindicated the earlier restraint. This carries a lesson in decision-making: knowing "where to stop" is as much a skill as knowing "how to win." One step too far often turns a clean victory into an open-ended quagmire. Real judgment shows in restraint, not expansion.
Q4: Why does the temptation to "just replace that person" keep failing?
From Iran to the Arab Spring, "topple the strongman ≠ solve the problem" plays out again and again — because bad outcomes are often structural: missing institutions, weak civil society, a power vacuum filled by whoever is best organized. Blaming a complex systemic problem on a single "bad man" is the laziest and most dangerous simplification. The real work isn't finding whom to topple, but patiently building the institutional foundation no one wants to build — unglamorous, yet the only thing that lasts.
